**SBI grounding for Growth Area #1:**

**Situation:** Following the H2 retention analysis that identified $200K in recoverable revenue from lapsed power users.

**Behavior:** Dana delivered the findings as a long Slack message in the product analytics channel, tagging the VP of Product. The message included the key numbers and recommendation but was formatted as running text with no summary, no clear ask, and no attached document. It appeared alongside dozens of other Slack messages that day.

**Impact:** The VP of Product later told the manager: "Dana's work is excellent but I sometimes miss it because it arrives as a wall of text in a thread." The retention findings were eventually incorporated into the board deck, but only after the manager re-packaged them into a structured memo. Dana did the analytical work that drove a board-level decision, but she did not get direct credit for it because the delivery format buried the insight. At L3, delivering the analysis is sufficient. At L4, ensuring the insight reaches the right audience in a format they can act on is part of the job.

**SBI grounding for Growth Area #2:**

**Situation:** During Q3, when the team had committed to delivering an A/B test analysis for the Growth team on a specific timeline.

**Behavior:** Dana identified that the team had 12 one-off dashboards that could be consolidated into 3 self-serve views. Without discussing the prioritization trade-off with her manager, she began the consolidation project alongside her other work. The dashboard consolidation took approximately two weeks of effort, during which the A/B test analysis was deprioritized.

**Impact:** The A/B test analysis was delivered two weeks late. The Growth team had planned a campaign launch around the test results and had to delay. The dashboard consolidation was genuinely valuable (40% reduction in ad-hoc requests), but the sequencing decision was Dana's alone. Her manager learned about the project after it was underway. At L3, this is a development area, not a performance problem: Dana's judgment about what matters was correct, but her judgment about when to act unilaterally vs. when to align with her manager was not. The risk is that as scope grows at L4, unilateral prioritization decisions have larger consequences.
