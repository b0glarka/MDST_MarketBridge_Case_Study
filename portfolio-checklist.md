Having weekly time estimates ground the stakeholder engagement model in reality. Even if these weekly time estimates are not strictly observed in practice, having a fixed time limit often forces participants into a more productive mindset. The decision hygiene section, with the RACI, Type 1 vs Type 2 framework and an escalation path, is where I'm most confident.  I tried to directly address how the previous Head of Analytics failed, because that experience can provide the clearest illustrations of the pitfalls that await my tenure in the role, and therefore this is what I need to avoid.

Compressing from that length to roughly 4 pages required cutting certain sections that I feel would have made the Manager OS structurally stronger. For example, the career ladder section is functional but thin. I would have liked to include more detailed level definitions with MarketBridge-specific behavioral examples. On the other hand, my writing tends to be more verbose than needed, not always to the benefit of the reader. As Blaise Pascal wrote - "I would have written a shorter letter, but I did not have the time.” Having the deadline looming forced a certain ruthless editing whose result, I hope, better reflects the purpose of the Manager OS.

## Reflection

Coming into this course, I was curious as to what was different about managing a data science team vs any other team in the workplace. I assumed that it was primarily about technical decisions (the right tools, the right pipelines, hiring for the right skills). In the end, however, the technical decisions are not particularly relevant in the team management context - competence is to some extent assumed, but due to technical debt,  budget and labor issues, technical decisions are constrained in many ways. Managing your team and representing your team within the company requires more universally applicable skills. In my invented example of the problems facing the Analytics team at Marketbridge, the transactions gap (487K vs. 523K) that made it in front of the board was in a fundamental sense not caused by technical problems in the pipeline. Rather, it was a human data governance problem that could have been resolved in a myriad of technical solutions - but it had not been identified explicitly and its results were not keenly felt until the board asked a very simple question.

The most immediately applicable idea I will be adopting is SBI. I have management experience (and have certainly been managed!) but my feedback has historically been more vague and generalizing than behavioral: "you need to communicate better" instead of (stealing from Dana's example) "in last week's metrics review, you delivered the analysis as a Slack message that the VP missed, which meant your work didn't get credited." I think this is due to fear of getting into the weeds litigating details, but also a fear of being "too mean." But the second form of feedback is actionable and specific in a way that the first is not. I need not wait to be a manager to implement this technique. I can evaluate my own performance (in assignments, presentations, exams) using this same technique, instead of just throwing up my hands and telling myself "Do Better!"

Still a work-in-progress - communicating effectively amidst the political dimensions of cross-functional leadership. The case study is effective (and fun!) because it identifies named stakeholders with clear motivations. In real orgs, identifying the players and their motives is more challenging - depending upon your role in the org, you may well have less exposure and insight to key stakeholders, but also there is ambiguity and contradiction that a case study doesn't replicate. So building up the needed relationships to get buy-in for proposed solution, whether technical or data governance related, when people's budgets and priorities may well sidestep my own concerns completely, that is a delicate and highly skilled task that I am just beginning to develop.
